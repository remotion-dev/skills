## Fear-based levers

Use when the reader is in pain or complacent. These activate threat-response cognition. Use sparingly and honestly — overuse breeds anxiety fatigue and the audience tunes out.

### Loss aversion

**What it activates:** The asymmetric pain of losing something already possessed (or implicitly promised) — roughly 2x the pleasure of gaining the equivalent.

**Fires when:** The reader has something to lose (status, position, time, opportunity, money already spent) and isn't yet acting to protect it.

**Pairs with:** Real urgency (the loss has a deadline), specificity (name what specifically is being lost).

**Fails when:** The reader has nothing to lose, or the loss isn't real, or the framing is melodramatic. Manufactured loss reads as manipulation.

### The Gap

**What it activates:** Competitive instinct — others are pulling ahead.

**Fires when:** The reader is in a competitive context (career, market, peer group) and the gap is real.

**Pairs with:** Cialdini's social proof (when the "others" can be specifically named), specificity (specific gap measurements beat vague ones).

**Fails when:** The audience isn't competitive (some industries, some life stages), the gap is invented, or the framing reads as fear-mongering.

### Time bankruptcy

**What it activates:** The exhaustion of running out of capacity. The reader is *already* doing too much.

**Fires when:** The audience is professionally squeezed — small-business owners, solopreneurs, parents, anyone with too many roles.

**Pairs with:** Compounding system lever (build it once, runs forever), friction removal, specificity ("20 minutes instead of 4 hours").

**Fails when:** The audience has time and is bored (use desire-based levers instead).

### Status threat

**What it activates:** Fear of being seen as the one who didn't get it, didn't keep up, fell behind their peer group.

**Fires when:** The audience cares about peer perception in this specific domain.

**Pairs with:** Identity reinforcement (you're already the kind of person who…), unity (your tribe is doing this).

**Fails when:** The status framing is invented, condescending, or comes from outside the audience's actual peer group. Status threats from a brand "above" the audience usually backfire.

### Cost of inaction

**What it activates:** The math of doing nothing — what doing nothing actually costs over time.

**Fires when:** There's a real, calculable cost to inaction. *"$40 per lead × 50 leads × 12 months = $24K/year"* hits harder than "expensive."

**Pairs with:** Specificity (mandatory — vague cost-of-inaction is just hand-waving), risk reversal ("here's how to stop the bleeding without committing").

**Fails when:** The math is fuzzy or invented. Numbers that aren't real are worse than no numbers.

## Desire-based levers

Use when the reader is curious or aspirational. These activate approach motivation. They convert better with warm/hot audiences than fear-based levers do.

### Gain framing

**What it activates:** Approach toward a specific outcome.

**Fires when:** The reader is in a curious or aspirational state and the gain is concrete.

**Pairs with:** Specificity (always — vague gain framing reads as hype), authority (when the gain is taught by a credible source).

**Fails when:** The gain is generic ("save time," "make more money"). Stage-5 sophistication markets are immune to abstract gain framing.

### Unfair advantage

**What it activates:** The desire to have something most don't.

**Fires when:** The offer genuinely is ahead of the curve and the audience can sense the edge is real.

**Pairs with:** Authority (here's why I'm telling you this first), curiosity gap (a glimpse of the edge before the ask).

**Fails when:** The advantage isn't actually unfair — every "secret" turns out to be widely known. Manufactured unfair advantages erode trust.

### The compounding system

**What it activates:** The desire for leverage — work that pays dividends without continuous effort.

**Fires when:** The audience is exhausted by maintenance work (the time-bankrupt audience especially) and the offer is system-shaped.

**Pairs with:** Time bankruptcy (the pain it relieves), specificity (compound at what rate, over what time).

**Fails when:** The "system" requires more maintenance than it removes. Audiences are wise to fake automation.

### Identity reinforcement

**What it activates:** The desire to act consistently with self-image. Cialdini's commitment/consistency principle, applied as a lever.

**Fires when:** The reader has an existing identity that the offer can confirm. *"You're already the marketing-forward agent in your market."*

**Pairs with:** Unity, peer signal, tribe markers.

**Fails when:** The identity attribution is wrong or flattering. The reader senses being told who they are when it isn't true.

### The vision

**What it activates:** Approach motivation through vivid future imagery.

**Fires when:** Long-form copy where Monroe's visualization step is in play.

**Pairs with:** Specificity (sensory, not abstract), gain framing (the vision is the gain made vivid).

**Fails when:** The vision is generic ("imagine financial freedom"). Visions need named days, named feelings, named details.

## Social levers (Cialdini's 7 principles)

The behavioral science backbone. Cialdini groups them into three categories.

### Reciprocity (relationship-building)

**What it activates:** The deep social rule that we repay what others give us.

**Fires when:** You give first, generously, before any ask. Most powerful when the gift is unexpected and personalized.

**Pairs with:** Liking (give first builds liking), authority (gifts from a credible source carry more weight).

**Fails when:** The gift is transparently transactional. *"Here's a free guide, now buy our course"* feels less like reciprocity and more like a setup.

### Commitment/consistency (action-driving)

**What it activates:** The drive to act in alignment with prior commitments and self-image.

**Fires when:** A small public yes paves the path to a larger one. Foot-in-the-door dynamics.

**Pairs with:** Identity reinforcement (the commitment shapes the identity), unity (committing publicly to a tribe).

**Fails when:** The early ask is too big — the principle requires the first commitment to be small enough to feel costless.

### Social proof (uncertainty-reducing)

**What it activates:** The shortcut of "what are people like me doing?"

**Fires when:** Specific, like-the-reader, plausible. *"3,000 agents already inside"* beats *"thousands of customers love it."*

**Pairs with:** Unity (the social proof comes from the reader's tribe), authority (proof from credible peers).

**Fails when:** Generic ("everyone loves us"), unverified ("rated #1 by users"), or from a peer group the reader doesn't identify with.

### Authority (uncertainty-reducing)

**What it activates:** The default trust we extend to credentialed expertise.

**Fires when:** The audience is uncertain and looking for a shortcut to confidence. Lead with authority for cold audiences who don't know you.

**Pairs with:** Specificity (the authority claim is specific — title, results, named affiliations), social proof (other authorities endorse).

**Fails when:** Authority is asserted vaguely. *"Industry expert"* triggers skepticism. *"Speaker at every Tom Ferry event for 4 years"* triggers credibility.

### Liking (relationship-building)

**What it activates:** Saying yes to those we like. Liking comes from similarity, compliments, and cooperative goals.

**Fires when:** Voice and tone do the work. Liking is mostly a side effect of writing well, not a direct lever to pull.

**Pairs with:** Voice work generally — Godin, Bernbach territory.

**Fails when:** Explicit "I like you" moves usually backfire. Liking has to be felt, not announced.

### Scarcity (action-driving)

**What it activates:** The urgency that comes from limited availability.

**Fires when:** The limit is real and named specifically. *"47 spots, 12 left"* beats *"limited spots available."*

**Pairs with:** Real urgency (scarcity is one type), commitment ("you committed to looking — now act before it's gone").

**Fails when:** Fake. Manufactured scarcity is the most common manipulation crime in marketing. Customers are increasingly literate about it.

### Unity (relationship-building, often the most powerful)

**What it activates:** Shared identity. *"For agents who…"* / *"If you're the kind of person who…"*

**Fires when:** Schwartz market sophistication stage 5 especially — when outcome-focused claims are exhausted, identity is the only entry left.

**Pairs with:** Identity reinforcement (mechanically the same lever, different angle), tribe markers (specific language and references).

**Fails when:** The unity claim is generic. *"For entrepreneurs"* is too broad. *"For agents who refuse to outsource their listing marketing"* is sharp enough to filter.

## Cognitive levers

Use to break the reader's existing thinking pattern.

### Curiosity gap

**What it activates:** The discomfort of an open loop. The brain seeks closure.

**Fires when:** The gap is specific and resolvable by the offer. Not clickbait — clickbait promises closure that isn't delivered.

**Pairs with:** Specificity (specific gaps work; vague ones don't), pattern interrupt (a contrarian frame opens a curiosity gap automatically).

**Fails when:** The gap is generic, dishonest, or the resolution underwhelms. Burned-out audiences detect curiosity gaps as a tactic and disengage.

### Pattern interrupt

**What it activates:** Re-engagement of attention by breaking the expected pattern.

**Fires when:** The audience has categorized this kind of message as ignorable. The pattern interrupt forces re-categorization.

**Pairs with:** Bernbach's anti-advertising tone (the interrupt sounds least like an ad), specificity (the new claim is concrete enough to take seriously).

**Fails when:** The interrupt becomes its own pattern. If every ad starts with *"Most people are doing this wrong,"* the contrarian opening becomes the new cliché.

### Specificity (the foundational lever)

**What it activates:** Cognitive trust. The brain treats specifics as evidence and abstractions as marketing.

**Fires when:** Always. Specificity is the universal upgrade — it makes every other lever sharper.

**Pairs with:** Everything.

**Fails when:** Specifics are invented. False precision is worse than honest abstraction.

### Anchoring

**What it activates:** The cognitive bias that the first number sets the reference frame for subsequent numbers.

**Fires when:** Comparing prices, costs, savings, or any quantitative claim. *"Most agents pay $40-60 per lead. Here's how to bring it under $5."*

**Pairs with:** Cost of inaction, gain framing with ROI.

**Fails when:** The anchor is implausible. Anchoring works only when the anchor itself is credible.

### Reframing

**What it activates:** A new lens on an existing fact.

**Fires when:** The audience has framed a situation in a less useful way. *"You're not behind. You're early."*

**Pairs with:** Authority (the reframe carries weight from a credible source), Voss's labeling (acknowledge the original frame before reframing).

**Fails when:** The reframe is forced or denies reality. Reframing isn't gaslighting — it has to be a true alternative interpretation.

## Action levers

Use to remove the last barrier between consideration and acting.

### Real urgency

**What it activates:** Action under deadline.

**Fires when:** The deadline is real — calendar, supply, schedule, market window. *"Spring market starts in 6 weeks"* hits when the audience operates on calendar logic.

**Pairs with:** Specificity (the reason is specific), scarcity (limited supply with a deadline).

**Fails when:** Fake. Fake urgency is the second-most-common manipulation crime after fake scarcity.

### Friction removal

**What it activates:** Action by reducing the perceived effort of the next step.

**Fires when:** The reader is convinced but stuck. The CTA names a small, specific, low-effort action.

**Pairs with:** Specificity (name the step), real urgency (do this small thing now).

**Fails when:** The friction-removal language doesn't match the actual experience. *"One-click signup"* that takes a 5-step form destroys trust.

### Risk reversal

**What it activates:** Disarms the "what if it doesn't work" block.

**Fires when:** The reader's primary block is trust or past disappointment. Money-back guarantees, free trials, no-contract language.

**Pairs with:** Authority (the brand is confident enough to guarantee), social proof (others took the risk and won).

**Fails when:** The guarantee is so caveated it isn't real. Risk reversal must actually reverse risk, not just appear to.

### Named-objection disarmament

**What it activates:** Trust by acknowledging.

**Fires when:** The reader has predictable objections and the copy names them out loud before the reader does.

**Pairs with:** Voss's labeling (mechanically the same move), risk reversal (disarm + remove the consequence).

**Fails when:** The objection is named but not actually addressed. Naming without disarming is worse than not naming.

## Identity levers

Use when self-concept is the door in. Especially powerful at Schwartz market sophistication stage 5.

### "You're already that person" reframe

**What it activates:** Acts as the identity-reinforcement lever, applied via reframe. Removes the implicit demand to change identity to act.

**Fires when:** The reader resists "you need to learn this" but accepts "you're already this kind of person."

**Pairs with:** Unity, peer signal.

**Fails when:** The identity claim is wrong. The reader knows when an identity is being projected onto them inaccurately.

### Peer signal

**What it activates:** The reader's awareness that their tribe is acting.

**Fires when:** The peer group is named accurately. *"3,000 agents like you are already inside."*

**Pairs with:** Cialdini social proof + unity stacked.

**Fails when:** The peer group is generic or doesn't match the reader's actual identification.

### Tribe markers

**What it activates:** Recognition. The reader sees themselves in the language.

**Fires when:** Specific words, references, or shorthand only the right reader recognizes. Strong filtering effect — Kennedy's filter principle expressed through vocabulary.

**Pairs with:** Unity, "for the kind of person who" framing.

**Fails when:** The markers are too inside or too generic. Both extremes break the move.

## Stickiness levers (when shareability matters)

Apply only when the content is meant to spread (organic social, word-of-mouth campaigns, viral hooks). Don't apply universally.

### Berger's STEPPS (from *Contagious*)

- **Social currency** — sharing this makes the sharer look good
- **Triggers** — top of mind cued by everyday context
- **Emotion** — high-arousal emotion (awe, anger, amusement; not sadness)
- **Public** — visibly used, observable
- **Practical value** — useful enough to share
- **Stories** — wrapped in narrative

### Heath brothers' SUCCESs (from *Made to Stick*)

- **Simple** — one core idea, distilled
- **Unexpected** — breaks an expectation
- **Concrete** — sensory, specific
- **Credible** — supported, sourced, plausible
- **Emotional** — feel something
- **Stories** — narrative carrier

For most persuasive copy (ads, sales pages, emails), don't apply these as primary levers — they're a checklist for shareability, not a foundation for conversion. Apply as a secondary check when shareability is part of the goal.
