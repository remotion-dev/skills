# Structural Frameworks — In Depth

Reference for `marketing-psychology` Step 2 (structure). Load this when the framework decision tree in SKILL.md isn't enough — when you need deeper guidance on applying a specific framework to the work at hand.

Three frameworks taught in depth, two acknowledged briefly.

---

## StoryBrand (Donald Miller) — the default for most marketing copy

Seven-part structure: *A character (the customer) has a problem, meets a guide (the brand), who gives them a plan, calls them to action, that helps them avoid failure and end in success.*

**Use when:** Audience is problem-aware or solution-aware. Most marketing copy fits here — landing pages, sales emails, About pages, mid-funnel content, web home pages.

**Strengths:**
- Forces customer-centricity by design.
- Forces clarity. The structure is built around answering "what do you offer, why should I care, what do I do next."
- Defaults to honest framing — customer is hero, brand is guide.

**Weaknesses:**
- Can become formulaic if applied mechanically. The seams show.
- Doesn't fit retention, internal communications, or activation copy.
- Can feel slow for short-form. Use Hook-Story-Offer instead for ads.

### Application

1. **Character** — name the customer. Not generic. *"Real estate agents in their first three years"* not *"agents."*

2. **Has a problem** — three layers:
   - **External problem** (the surface issue): *"Their listings sit on the market too long."*
   - **Internal feeling** (how the external problem makes them feel): *"They feel like they're losing the trust of their sellers."*
   - **Philosophical stake** (why this matters in a bigger sense): *"It's not fair that the agents who actually do the work get out-marketed by the ones who just advertise more."*

3. **Meets a guide** — empathy + authority. Empathy comes first. *"I've been there. I lost three listings my first year because I didn't know how to market them."* Then authority. *"Since then, I've built marketing systems for over 3,000 agents."*

4. **Who gives them a plan** — usually three steps. Concrete. Not "we'll work together." *"1) Pick the lecture that matches your current bottleneck. 2) Run the system once with one listing. 3) Repeat with the next listing."*

5. **And calls them to action** — direct. Don't soften. *"Join AiM."* Two CTAs is fine — direct CTA + transitional CTA (e.g., "Get the free guide") for those not yet ready.

6. **That helps them avoid failure** — name what happens if they don't act. Concrete. *"Your listings keep sitting. Your sellers keep calling."*

7. **And ends in success** — paint the win. Specific, vivid. *"Listings move. Sellers refer. Your marketing runs without you."*

### When StoryBrand fits poorly

- The reader doesn't see themselves as having a problem (use a different opening — Schwartz unaware-stage approach).
- The reader is most-aware (StoryBrand front-loads problem-naming, which slows down a hot lead).
- The piece is too short for the seven beats (use Hook-Story-Offer or PAS).

## Monroe's Motivated Sequence — the default for long-form persuasion

Five-step structure designed for moving an audience the full distance from awareness to action in one piece: **Attention → Need → Satisfaction → Visualization → Action.**

Originally developed in the 1930s for speech but it's the spine underneath modern sales pages, webinars, video sales letters, and direct-response talks.

**Use when:** You have time and space. Sales pages, webinars, VSLs, keynote talks, listing presentations, pitch decks, long-form video scripts.

**Strengths:**
- Built for the full journey from cold to action.
- The visualization step is what most other frameworks lack — it's the conversion engine.
- Natively emotional in structure.

**Weaknesses:**
- Too heavy for short copy.
- Skipping any step weakens the whole piece.
- Easy to over-extend the Need section and lose the reader before satisfaction lands.

### Application

1. **Attention** — break pattern, earn the read. Stop the scroll. Fill the room. This is where pre-framing lives. Don't open with "today I want to talk about." Open with a question, story, contrarian claim, or vivid scene.

2. **Need** — name and dimensionalize the problem. Make them feel it. Not just "you have a problem" but "this is what the problem is doing to your life." Specifics. The reader has to think *"yes, that's exactly it."* If you skip this and go straight to the solution, they don't yet feel the cost of inaction.

3. **Satisfaction** — present the solution. Show how it solves the need specifically. Make the connection between the pain in step 2 and the relief in step 3 explicit. *"Remember that thing you said you couldn't keep up with? Here's how this handles that."*

4. **Visualization** — paint the future. Make them see, feel, and inhabit the outcome. Concrete sensory detail. *"Imagine sitting down Monday morning, and your week of content is already done."* This is the step most writers skip and the step that converts. Without visualization, the reader knows the solution exists but hasn't decided they want to live in the world where they have it.

5. **Action** — direct, specific call. Tell them exactly what to do next. Not "learn more" — "click the button below, fill in your email, and you'll get the first lecture in your inbox in 30 seconds."

### When Monroe fits poorly

- The piece is short. Use Hook-Story-Offer.
- The audience is already most-aware. Skip steps 1-2, go straight to action with a specific offer.
- The piece is informational, not persuasive. Don't impose Monroe on a how-to article — it'll feel manipulative.

## Hook-Story-Offer (Russell Brunson) — the default for short, high-energy direct response

Three-part compression: **Hook stops the scroll. Story creates the curiosity gap or names the pain. Offer collapses friction.**

**Use when:** You need momentum fast. Short ads, video hooks under 30 seconds, short emails, social posts with a CTA, push notifications with substance.

**Strengths:**
- Designed for the modern attention economy.
- Forces a real offer (not just a topic).
- Compresses well. The same skeleton works at 50 words and 500.

**Weaknesses:**
- Lightweight for product-aware audiences who need disarmament.
- Can feel pitchy if the hook is weak or the offer is generic.

### Application

1. **Hook** — first 3-7 words. Filter, surprise, or name the reader. Stop the scroll.

   Strong hooks:
   - Filter: *"Real estate agents who hate writing listing descriptions:"*
   - Specificity: *"3 prompts. 30 minutes. A month of Reels."*
   - Contrarian: *"Stop using ChatGPT for social media."*
   - Curiosity: *"There's one prompt I run before every listing presentation."*

   Weak hooks: anything starting with "Are you tired of," "Imagine if," "What if I told you," "Discover the secret to."

2. **Story** — one specific moment, problem, or insight. Not the full case — just enough to open the loop. The reader should feel they need to know more.

   Strong story moves:
   - One specific scene: *"It was 9pm, I was sitting at my kitchen table trying to write a listing description for the 14th time that month."*
   - One specific insight: *"Here's the thing nobody tells you about AI for real estate marketing."*
   - One specific outcome: *"30 days later, I had 9 listing appointments from the system."*

3. **Offer** — the specific thing they get if they act. Free + specific + now beats vague any day.

   Strong offer mechanics:
   - Name what they get exactly.
   - Name how they get it (link, click, sign up).
   - Name the time cost ("45-minute training," "5-minute read").
   - Name the price (especially "free").

### When Hook-Story-Offer fits poorly

- The reader has serious objections that need to be addressed (use Monroe or StoryBrand to give space).
- The piece is informational, not persuasive (use information design instead of forcing a framework).
- The "story" would feel forced (sometimes a hook + offer is enough — don't fabricate a story for the sake of structure).

## PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solve) — quick mental shortcut

Three-beat compression: **Problem. Agitate. Solve.**

Useful when you need something faster than StoryBrand and shorter than Hook-Story-Offer is willing to be.

**Use when:** Quick troubleshooting copy, support content, FAQ-style content, very short emails. Anywhere the reader is problem-aware and the offer is the fix.

**Application:**

1. **Problem** — name what hurts. *"Your listings sit on the market."*
2. **Agitate** — make it more vivid. *"Sellers calling. Showings dropping. The other agent in the area is closing deals."*
3. **Solve** — offer the path. *"Here's the system that fixes this."*

PAS is mostly superseded by StoryBrand for most cases. Worth knowing for short-form troubleshooting where the structure still serves.

## AIDA — acknowledged

**Attention → Interest → Desire → Action.**

The grandfather of persuasion frameworks. Largely superseded by Monroe (which adds the visualization step) and StoryBrand (which forces customer-centricity).

Mentioned here only because the term still appears in marketing literature. Not taught in depth — use Monroe instead, which does what AIDA does plus the conversion step AIDA lacks.

## Picking the framework — the decision tree (repeated from SKILL.md)

```
Is this short copy with one job (ad, post, micro-CTA)?
  YES → Hook-Story-Offer
  NO ↓

Is this long-form, moving someone from cold to committed in one piece (sales page, webinar, talk)?
  YES → Monroe's Motivated Sequence
  NO ↓

Does the customer have a problem the offer solves?
  YES → StoryBrand
  NO ↓

Is this troubleshooting/support/quick-fix content?
  YES → PAS
  NO → No framework needed; structure with information design + selective levers
```

When in doubt between StoryBrand and Monroe: StoryBrand if the piece has a defined problem-solution shape, Monroe if you're moving the reader the full distance from "I haven't thought about this" to "I'm acting now." Monroe is the longer journey; StoryBrand is the cleaner shape.
