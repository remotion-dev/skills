# Named Persuasive Tasks — In Depth

Reference for `marketing-psychology` when the task involves pre-framing or objection handling. Both are high-value moves most writers skip.

---

## Pre-framing (Cialdini's pre-suasion)

**The principle:** What happens *before* the message lands often matters more than the message itself. The opening seconds prime the reader's mental state. Set the right frame and the message lands on prepared ground. Set the wrong frame and the message bounces, no matter how well-written the body.

Cialdini's work in *Pre-Suasion* showed that the most persuasive moments often happen *before* any explicit ask. A question, image, or concept introduced 30 seconds before the message changes how the message is received.

### The five pre-framing moves

**Question first.** Open with the question the message answers. The reader's mind reaches for an answer; you fill that reach with the message.

> *Without pre-framing: "Here's a faster way to write listing descriptions."*
>
> *With pre-framing: "How long does it take you to write a listing description that doesn't sound like every other agent's? Here's a faster way."*

**Concept priming.** Surface a related idea before the pitch. An ad with a "freedom" image before a financial product converts differently than the same product without it. The image primes the receptive concept.

> *In copy: "There's a moment, the morning after a listing goes live, when you can tell whether your marketing did its job. The phone rings or it doesn't."*

**Temporal framing.** Move the reader's attention to a specific moment before delivering the message. They visualize the moment first; the message lands inside that visualization.

> *"You know that moment, the night before a listing goes live, when you sit down to write the description and your mind goes blank?"*

**Identity priming.** Remind the reader who they are before asking them to act. The action becomes consistent with the identity.

> *"For agents who refuse to fall behind. Here's the system."*

**Permission priming.** Give the reader explicit permission to want what's coming. Removes shame, reluctance, or self-judgment that might block reception.

> *"It's okay to admit you've been frustrated with AI tools. Most agents who tried them are."*

### Where pre-framing lives in copy

- The first line of an ad
- The subject line of an email (pre-frames the body before they open it)
- The opening hook of a video
- The Attention step in Monroe's Motivated Sequence
- The first 3 seconds of any persuasive interaction
- The lead-in to a CTA (pre-frames the click)

### Test for pre-framing

> *Does the opening prime the reader to be receptive to what comes next? Or does it just announce what's coming?*

Announcing kills the move. Priming makes the move work.

### Failure mode: pre-frame mismatch

Pre-framing crosses into manipulation when the frame doesn't honestly match what's being delivered. Priming "freedom" before pitching a restrictive contract is dishonest. Priming "this isn't another sales pitch" before delivering a sales pitch is dishonest. The pre-frame must be true to the offer.

If the body of the message can't deliver on what the pre-frame promised, rewrite the pre-frame, not the body.

## Objection handling

**The principle:** The reader has objections before they finish reading. Naming and disarming the objections before they fully form is the persuasive move. Pretending the objections don't exist is the failure.

Most writers either ignore objections (hoping the reader won't notice) or address them defensively (reading as protest-too-much). The right move is proactive — name the objection more clearly than the reader has named it themselves, then disarm it.

### The mini-framework: Acknowledge → Reframe → Evidence

**1. Acknowledge.** Say the objection out loud. Voss's *labeling* technique:

- *"It seems like…"*
- *"Probably you're thinking…"*
- *"Most people in your position would assume…"*
- *"You might be wondering…"*

The acknowledge step does two things: (1) signals that you understand the reader's actual mental state, which builds trust; (2) names the objection while it's still vague in the reader's mind, which keeps the reader from articulating it sharper than you can.

**2. Reframe.** Offer the new lens. Not denial — a true alternative interpretation.

The reframe must be honest. *"This isn't a problem because…"* is denial. *"Here's why this isn't actually the issue you think it is…"* is reframe.

**3. Evidence.** Specific proof for the reframe. Numbers, examples, named outcomes, real testimonials, demonstrable claims.

Without evidence, the reframe is just an assertion. With evidence, the reframe is grounded.

### Common objections and the reframe pattern

**"This won't work for me / my market is different"**

- *Acknowledge:* "I get it — every agent's market feels specific."
- *Reframe:* "What I've seen across 3,000 members is that the system's bones work everywhere; what changes is the local adaptation, not the system itself."
- *Evidence:* "Here's an agent in [specific small market] using it. Here's an agent in [specific large market] using it. Same bones, different surface."

**"I don't have time"**

- *Acknowledge:* "Most people I talk to say the same thing."
- *Reframe:* "And that's exactly why this works the way it does — it's designed for people who don't have time to learn another system."
- *Evidence:* "The whole thing is one rule. The training is 6 minutes. You'll know if it's right for you in the first three."

**"It's too expensive"**

- *Acknowledge:* "$199/month is real money."
- *Reframe:* "Here's the math compared to what most agents are already spending on tools that aren't paying them back."
- *Evidence:* "[Specific cost comparison.]"

**"I've tried this before / been burned before"**

- *Acknowledge:* "If you've taken AI training before that didn't deliver, that experience is real."
- *Reframe:* "Here's what's specifically different about this approach."
- *Evidence:* "[Mechanism difference, named.] [Result, named.]"

**"I'm not ready"**

- *Acknowledge:* "Nobody feels fully ready."
- *Reframe:* "Readiness isn't something you achieve before starting; it's something you build by starting."
- *Evidence:* "[Example of someone who started without being ready.]"

**"I need to think about it"**

- *Acknowledge:* "Of course — this is a real decision."
- *Reframe:* "While you're thinking, here's what to think about specifically."
- *Evidence:* "[The actual decision criteria, named, so the reader can evaluate honestly.]"

### Where objection handling lives in copy

- Sales pages — almost always a dedicated objections section, often near the bottom
- Long-form emails — after the offer, before the CTA
- The Need or Visualization step of Monroe's Motivated Sequence
- Mid-paragraph in any longer copy when an objection would naturally form
- FAQ sections (objections in question form)
- Sales conversations — Voss's tactical empathy applied live

### Test for objection handling

> *Did I name the objection more clearly than the reader had named it themselves?*

If yes, the disarm works. The reader feels seen, then walked through the reframe.

If the reader can articulate the objection sharper than the copy did, the disarm misses. The reader will walk away with the objection still active.

### Failure modes specific to objection handling

**Acknowledged but not reframed.** *"You might think this is too expensive. It's not."* The reader has been told their objection isn't valid without being given a new lens. Trust drops.

**Reframed but not evidenced.** *"It's not too expensive — here's why."* Followed by an assertion, not proof. Reads as protest.

**Strawman objections.** Naming objections the reader doesn't actually have. The reader thinks: *"Why are you bringing this up?"* and trust drops.

**Defensive tone.** Objection handling delivered as defense reads as protest-too-much. Best objection handling sounds like the brand is calmly walking the reader through the obvious questions, not arguing.

## Pre-framing + objection handling stacked

The two moves stack well in long-form copy.

**Pre-framing in the opening:** sets the right mental state.
**Objection handling in the middle/end:** disarms what the reader develops as they read.

Together, they bookend the persuasive piece — primed reception at the start, disarmed resistance at the close.

### Worked stack example

**Pre-frame (opening):**
> *"There's a moment that decides whether your marketing works or not — the morning after a listing goes live. The phone rings or it doesn't. The showings book or they don't. Most agents are guessing. A few have a system."*

**Body:**
> [The actual pitch — what the system is, how it works, what the agent gets.]

**Objection handling (closing):**
> *"You might be thinking, 'I've tried AI training before, it didn't change anything.' I get it. Most AI training is theory — what AI is, how it works, where it's going. None of that gets you a listing. The reason this works differently is that it skips the theory entirely. You sit down for a 45-minute training, you copy three prompts, you have content for a month. That's it. That's the whole thing."*

The pre-frame primed the moment-after-listing-goes-live frame. The objection handling closed the loop on the reader's most likely block. Both honest. Both grounded.

This stack is the spine of most high-converting long-form copy.
