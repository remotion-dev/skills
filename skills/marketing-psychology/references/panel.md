# The Panel — 11 Minds in Depth

Reference for `marketing-psychology` Step 4 (panel check). Load this when the panel cheat sheet in SKILL.md isn't enough — when you need the deeper lens of a specific mind, their anchor moves, and the test they apply.

Each entry: who they are, what they own, their lens, when to consult, anchor moves, the test they apply.

---

## David Ogilvy — the headline standard

**Owns:** Headlines, specificity, research-backed claims, respect for the reader.

**Lens:** *"On the average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy."* If the headline doesn't earn the read, nothing else matters. Specificity over abstraction. The consumer is not a moron.

**Consult on:** Headlines, the first line of any copy, claims that need to feel earned.

**Anchor moves:**
- Be specific. Use numbers.
- Treat the reader as intelligent.
- Test relentlessly — let data, not opinion, decide.
- The headline is 80% of the work.

**Test he applies:** *"Does this headline earn the body? If the reader stops here, did they get something useful?"*

---

## Donald Miller — the structure

**Owns:** StoryBrand framework. Customer-as-hero. Clarity over cleverness.

**Lens:** Customer is the hero. Brand is the guide. If the brand is the hero of its own marketing, the marketing fails. Clarity beats cleverness.

**Consult on:** Structure of substantive copy. Auditing whether you've made yourself the hero.

**Anchor moves:**
- Name the customer's problem first (external problem, internal feeling, philosophical stake).
- Position yourself as the guide — empathy + authority.
- Give a clear plan, usually three steps.
- Call to action — direct, low-friction.
- Name the stakes — what success looks like, what failure looks like.

**Test he applies:** *"In 5 seconds, can the reader tell me what you offer, why they should care, and what to do next?"*

---

## Russell Brunson — direct-response mechanics

**Owns:** Hook-Story-Offer. Curiosity loops. Real CTAs.

**Lens:** Every persuasive piece is hook-story-offer compressed. The hook stops the scroll. The story opens the loop. The offer collapses friction. He's also the source of the "perfect webinar" mechanic — useful when an offer is the conversion point.

**Consult on:** Short-form direct response. Offers that need to feel inevitable. Anything where momentum matters more than depth.

**Anchor moves:**
- Real offer = free + specific + now.
- Open a curiosity loop that's resolvable by the offer (not clickbait).
- CTA names the action, not "learn more."
- Stack a value reveal before the ask.

**Test he applies:** *"Is there an actual offer, or just a topic? What does the reader get if they click?"*

---

## Seth Godin — the voice

**Owns:** Voice, remarkability, sentence cadence, permission marketing.

**Lens:** Short. True. Remarkable. No filler. No foreplay. Permission-based — earn the reader's attention, don't extract it.

**Consult on:** Voice audit. Cutting filler. Anything that risks sounding corporate.

**Anchor moves:**
- Cut the warmup.
- Cut the qualifier.
- Cut the throat-clear.
- Say the thing.

**Test he applies:** *"Would this survive being read aloud with no filler?"*

---

## Phil M. Jones — exact-language transitions

**Owns:** Bridge phrases that move people from one mental state to the next. The exact words that work in sales conversations and persuasive copy.

**Lens:** Specific phrases are not decoration; they're bridges. *"What most people do is…"* / *"The good news is…"* / *"Here's what nobody tells you…"* / *"Imagine if…"* / *"Just so I'm clear…"* — each one preps a specific receptive state in the listener.

**Consult on:** Transitions inside copy. Setting up reframes or payoffs. Sales conversation patterns. Anywhere two ideas need to connect without sounding jammed together.

**Anchor moves:**
- Earn the next sentence with a bridge.
- Set up the payoff before delivering it.
- Don't pivot cold; prep with a phrase.
- The right phrase makes the listener want to hear what comes next.

**Test he applies:** *"Did I earn the transition, or did I just jam two ideas together?"*

---

## Dan Kennedy — message-market-match and the filter

**Owns:** Calling the reader by name. Filtering in the right reader, filtering out the wrong one. Message-market-match.

**Lens:** Great direct response *filters*. The hook should make the wrong reader keep scrolling. The right reader should feel called out. Real urgency works; fake urgency destroys credibility forever.

**Consult on:** Hooks. Audience-naming lines. Urgency claims. Cold-traffic copy.

**Anchor moves:**
- Name the reader explicitly when targeting is broad.
- Use specifics that filter (income level, profession, situation, identity).
- Urgency rooted in real reasons — calendar, supply, market window.
- "Attention [specific reader]:" still works because it's honest filtering.

**Test he applies:** *"Does this hook filter — does the right reader stop and the wrong one scroll past?"*

---

## Bill Bernbach — wit and anti-advertising honesty

**Owns:** The unexpected truth. Sounding least like an ad. Wit as credibility.

**Lens:** *"Think Small."* *"We try harder."* The honest line beats the clever line. The brand that admits the obvious thing nobody else will say earns trust. Wit is not decoration — wit is honesty delivered with confidence.

**Consult on:** Tone audit. Contrarian hooks. Anything risking ad-speak.

**Anchor moves:**
- Say the obvious true thing (especially the inconvenient one).
- Use dry tone — let the wit emerge from the truth, not the wordplay.
- Resist the temptation to sound smart.
- An ad that sounds least like an ad earns trust fastest.

**Test he applies:** *"Does this sound like a person, or a brand?"*

---

## Robert Cialdini — the academic backbone

**Owns:** The 7 principles of influence. The behavioral science behind why levers work.

**Lens:** Reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity, unity. Cialdini groups them by purpose — relationship-building (unity, liking, reciprocity), uncertainty-reducing (social proof, authority), action-driving (consistency, scarcity).

**Consult on:** Lever selection rationale. Sales pages with multiple persuasive elements. Anything that needs to feel rigorously grounded in behavioral science.

**Anchor moves:**
- Match the principle to the blocking force.
- Layer principles when long-form; lead with one when short.
- Unity is the most powerful — shared identity overrides most other principles.
- Pre-suasion: what happens *before* the message often matters more than the message.

**Test he applies:** *"If a behavioral economist read this, would they spot the principle in use, or would it feel like a coincidence?"*

---

## Eugene Schwartz — the diagnostic

**Owns:** Awareness stages. Market sophistication. The principle that copy channels existing desire rather than creating it.

**Lens:** *"Copy cannot create desire. It can only channel the desires of the masses."* The reader's awareness stage and the market's sophistication stage determine everything else. Most copy fails because the writer is at the wrong stage.

**Consult on:** The diagnostic step (Step 1). Auditing whether you're writing to the wrong stage. Markets that feel saturated.

**Anchor moves:**
- Diagnose first. Always.
- Match the message to the stage — not where you wish the reader were.
- At market sophistication stage 5, lead with identity, not outcome.
- Find the existing desire and name it more precisely than the reader has named it themselves.

**Test he applies:** *"Am I writing to where the reader actually is, or to where I wish they were?"*

---

## Chris Voss — tactical empathy

**Owns:** Mirroring. Labeling. Calibrated questions. Sales-side persuasion and objection handling.

**Lens:** The other side wants to feel understood. Negotiation isn't about winning — it's about collaborating against the situation. Tactical empathy is intentionally using neuroscience to influence emotions. The adversary is the situation, not the person.

**Consult on:** Sales conversations. Objection handling. Listing presentations. Any one-on-one persuasive context. Long-form copy with objection sections.

**Anchor moves:**
- Label the emotion before pitching: *"It seems like…"* / *"Probably you're thinking…"* / *"Most people in your position…"*
- Mirror the last few words to keep them talking and signal listening.
- Ask calibrated *how* and *what* questions, not *why*. (Why feels like accusation.)
- Silence is a tool. After a label, let it sit.

**Test he applies:** *"Did I name what they're feeling before I asked them to act?"*

---

## Al Ries (with Jack Trout) — positioning and category

**Owns:** Positioning. Category strategy. The macro layer of how markets form and how brands win in them.

**Lens:** *The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing.* Be first in a category, or invent a category you can be first in. Marketing is a battle of perception, not products. The mind has limited slots per category — get into a slot or invent a new category.

**Consult on:** Positioning lines. Category framing. Anytime the work has to define what *kind* of thing it is in a crowded market.

**Anchor moves:**
- Find the empty category. If none exists, invent one.
- Lead with the position, not the features.
- Don't try to compete on every axis — compete on the axis nobody else owns.
- The first brand in a category usually wins; if you can't be first in your category, redefine the category until you are.

**Test he applies:** *"What category does this own in the reader's mind? If none, the reader has nowhere to file it."*

---

## How to load the panel

Don't run all 11 every time. Load by stage of work:

- **Headlines, hooks** → Ogilvy, Kennedy, Bernbach
- **Structure** → Miller, Brunson, Schwartz
- **Voice** → Godin, Bernbach
- **Transitions** → Phil M. Jones
- **Levers / persuasive grounding** → Cialdini, Schwartz
- **Sales conversation, objection handling** → Voss, Phil M. Jones
- **Positioning, category** → Ries

If a draft fails any consulted panel member's test, rewrite that piece before moving on.
