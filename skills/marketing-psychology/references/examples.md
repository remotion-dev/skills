# Worked Examples

Reference for `marketing-psychology` calibration. Audience-agnostic examples demonstrating the five-step sequence applied to real persuasive tasks. The point is calibration of the *process*, not borrowing the language.

Load this when you want to see the sequence applied end-to-end before writing.

---

## Example 1: A short ad for a productivity course

### Diagnose

- **Awareness:** solution-aware (knows productivity systems exist, hasn't picked one)
- **Sophistication:** stage 5 (productivity advice is exhausted)
- **Emotional state:** fatigue ("another productivity pitch")
- **Blocking force:** past disappointment ("I've tried these systems before")
- **Temperature:** cold

### Structure

Hook-Story-Offer (short ad).

### Lever

- **Dominant:** Identity (stage 5 calls for it)
- **Supporting:** Specificity

### Draft

> For people who've tried every productivity system and given up.
>
> I built one rule. Not five. Not seventeen. One.
>
> Here's the rule, free, no signup. Read it in 90 seconds.

### Panel check

- **Kennedy filter:** yes — "for people who've tried every system and given up" filters in the right reader
- **Ogilvy specificity:** yes — "one rule," "90 seconds"
- **Bernbach tone:** yes — admits the obvious thing (productivity systems usually fail)
- **Brunson offer:** yes — free, specific, no signup
- **Godin cadence:** yes — short, sharp, no filler

### Failure audit

- Hype? No, no clichés.
- Manipulation? No, the offer matches.
- Formula? Hook-Story-Offer is showing slightly. Acceptable for short copy.
- Wrong stage? No, identity-first matches stage 5.
- Pile-on? Two levers, complementary. Pass.

## Example 2: A poor draft, then the rewrite

### Original (poor)

> Are you tired of struggling with productivity? Discover our revolutionary new system that will transform the way you work and unlock your full potential. Don't miss out — sign up today!

### What's wrong (audit)

- "Are you tired of" — dead opening, no filter (failure mode #7: unfiltered hook)
- "revolutionary new system" — stage-2 claim in a stage-5 market (failure mode #4: wrong-stage writing)
- "transform the way you work" — abstract, generic (failure mode #1: hype)
- "unlock your full potential" — banned phrase, hype (failure mode #1)
- "Don't miss out" — fake urgency (failure mode #2: manipulation)
- No specifics, no real offer (failure mode #9: no real offer)
- Could be selling literally any productivity product

### Rewrite (using the sequence)

Diagnosis: same as Example 1.
Structure: Hook-Story-Offer.
Lever: identity + specificity.
Panel: filter the hook, cut the hype, add the specific.

> For people who've quit three productivity systems this year.
>
> The reason isn't you. The reason is they all assume you have time you don't have.
>
> One rule. Three minutes to read. No app, no system, no signup.

Same skeleton. Calibrated copy.

The difference isn't talent. The difference is the process — diagnose, pick the right framework, pick the right lever, audit the draft for failure modes.

## Example 3: A pre-framing hook

### Without pre-framing

> *"Here's a faster way to write listing descriptions."*

This announces. It tells the reader what's coming. It's information, not persuasion.

### With pre-framing

> *"You know that moment, the night before a listing goes live, when you sit down to write the description and your mind goes blank?*
>
> *Here's a faster way."*

The first version announces. The second primes the specific moment, then delivers. The reader is *already in the moment* when the offer arrives — the offer lands on prepared ground.

The pre-frame uses temporal framing (specific moment) + permission priming (it's okay that your mind goes blank). The body of the offer didn't change. The reception did.

## Example 4: Objection handling in long-form

A sales page section addressing the "I don't have time" objection.

### Acknowledge

> *Most people I talk to say the same thing: "I'm too busy to learn another system right now."*

This is Voss's labeling. Names the objection out loud, in the reader's own likely framing. Trust builds.

### Reframe

> *I understand. And that's exactly why this works the way it does — it's designed for people who don't have time to learn. The whole thing is one rule. The training is 6 minutes. You'll know if it's right for you in the first three.*

The reframe doesn't deny the objection — it accepts the time pressure as real and offers a true alternative interpretation: *the system is designed for the constraint you're naming.*

### Evidence

> *I've used it in airports, in line for coffee, between client calls. If 6 minutes once is too much time, this isn't the right thing — and that's a fair conclusion to come to in 90 seconds, not after a 30-day trial.*

Specific evidence. The last sentence is unusual — it gives the reader an exit. Paradoxically, this *increases* trust because it signals that the brand isn't trying to retain readers who genuinely don't fit.

The full move (acknowledge → reframe → evidence) is what most copy lacks. Most copy either ignores objections or gets defensive about them. This pattern walks the reader through the doubt and lands them, calmly, on the other side.

## Example 5: Pre-framing + objection handling stacked in a longer piece

The two moves stack well. Pre-framing in the opening sets reception. Objection handling in the body or close disarms what develops.

### Pre-frame (opening)

> *"There's a moment that decides whether your marketing works or not — the morning after a listing goes live. The phone rings or it doesn't. The showings book or they don't. Most agents are guessing. A few have a system."*

(Temporal framing + identity priming + the Gap lever in the last sentence.)

### Body

> *[The actual pitch — what the system is, how it works, what the reader gets if they act.]*

### Objection handling (close)

> *"You might be thinking, 'I've tried AI training before, it didn't change anything.' I get it. Most AI training is theory — what AI is, how it works, where it's going. None of that gets you a listing.*
>
> *The reason this works differently is that it skips the theory entirely. You sit down for a 45-minute training, you copy three prompts, you have content for a month. That's it. That's the whole thing."*

(Acknowledge → reframe → evidence — and the reframe identifies the *specific mechanism difference* between what failed before and what's offered now.)

The pre-frame primed the moment-after-listing-goes-live frame. The objection handling closed the loop on the most likely block.

This stack is the spine of most high-converting long-form copy.

## Example 6: A subject line for a re-engagement email (micro-copy)

### Diagnose

- **Awareness:** product-aware (lapsed customer, knows the offer)
- **Sophistication:** stage 5
- **Emotional state:** apathy (let it lapse, not actively unhappy)
- **Blocking force:** wrong moment ("not now")
- **Temperature:** cold-warm (knows you, hasn't been engaged)

### Structure

None — too short for a framework. Apply diagnosis + lever + audit only.

### Lever

- **Dominant:** Curiosity gap
- **Supporting:** none — pile-on for a subject line is fatal

### Draft (5 options to test)

1. *"You probably forgot about this."*
2. *"Quick question — was it the price, the time, or something else?"*
3. *"Nothing salesy here."*
4. *"One thing changed since you cancelled."*
5. *"Not asking you to come back."*

### Panel check (Ogilvy + Kennedy only — micro-copy)

- Ogilvy specificity: 1, 4 strong; 3, 5 vague
- Kennedy filter: 2 filters by surfacing the actual reason; 4 filters by surfacing the change
- Best two: 2 and 4

### Failure audit

- Hype? No.
- Manipulation? Watch 5 — "not asking you to come back" is true only if it actually is, otherwise it's bait.
- Wrong stage? No.

This is the appropriate level of process for micro-copy. Diagnosis + lever + light audit. Skipping the framework and panel-check steps because they're overkill for the format.

## How to use these examples

Use them as calibration of the *process*, not as a copy library to borrow language from. The point is:

- The diagnosis comes first.
- The framework is picked from diagnosis.
- The lever is picked from diagnosis (specifically the blocking force).
- The panel checks the work.
- The audit catches what slipped through.

Run the same process on the actual task at hand. The output will be specific to the audience, the offer, and the moment — but the sequence is the same every time.
