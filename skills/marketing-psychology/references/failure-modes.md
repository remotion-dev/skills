# Failure Modes — In Depth

Reference for `marketing-psychology` Step 5 (failure audit). The 11 most common ways persuasive copy goes wrong, with symptom, cause, and fix for each.

Use this when the failure mode table in SKILL.md isn't enough — when you've spotted a problem and need deeper diagnosis.

---

## 1. Hype

**Symptom:** Reads like every other ad in the category. Generic outcomes, generic benefits, no specifics. Could be selling literally any product in the same space. Banned-words density is high (transform, revolutionize, unlock, game-changer, secrets, master, dominate, 10x, next-level).

**Cause:** Reaching for clichés instead of levers. Tactical thinking without strategic grounding. Often happens when the writer can't answer the diagnostic — without knowing the reader's state, they default to category-standard phrasing.

**Fix:**
- Replace abstract claims with specific ones. *"Transform your marketing"* → *"30 Reels in 90 minutes."*
- Cut every word that could appear in any other ad in the category.
- Run the Bernbach test: does this sound like a person, or a brand?
- Re-do the diagnostic if the writer can't ground the copy in a specific reader state.

---

## 2. Manipulation

**Symptom:** The lever doesn't honestly match the offer. Fake urgency, invented scarcity, fabricated outcomes. Reads as effective at first but erodes trust on a second read or after the buyer experiences the offer.

**Cause:** Reaching for shortcuts instead of doing the diagnostic work. Pressure to convert overrides the integrity check.

**Fix:**
- Apply the litmus test: *if the reader saw exactly how this lever was being used, would they still feel the message was honest?*
- If no, replace the lever with one that honestly fits.
- Specifically: kill fake countdowns, invented scarcity, generic testimonials, income claims without qualifiers, and urgency without a real reason.
- Real persuasion compounds. Manipulation works once.

---

## 3. Formula

**Symptom:** The framework is showing through. Reader can sense the structure imposed on the message. Reads like it was written from a template — predictable beats, predictable sentence shapes, predictable transitions.

**Cause:** Applying a framework mechanically instead of using it as a spine. The framework becomes the message instead of the structure beneath the message.

**Fix:**
- Disguise the seams. Frameworks should feel inevitable, not imposed.
- Vary sentence length aggressively in each section.
- Break the pattern at one point in each section — a fragment, a question, an aside.
- If the StoryBrand "guide reveal" is happening exactly where StoryBrand says it should and reads as such, rewrite the reveal to feel earned, not slotted.

---

## 4. Wrong-stage writing

**Symptom:** Copy is doing more (or less) than the audience needs. Either over-explaining what they already know, or assuming knowledge they don't have. Reader either gets bored (over-explained) or confused (under-explained).

**Cause:** Skipping the diagnostic. Writing to where you wish the audience were instead of where they actually are. Most common in copy written by experts who forget what it's like not to know what they know.

**Fix:**
- Re-do the diagnostic — specifically Q1 (awareness stage) and Q2 (sophistication stage).
- Adjust the depth of explanation to match the actual stage.
- Most-aware audiences need offer + friction removal, not problem-naming.
- Unaware audiences need stories and indirect framing, not direct offers.

---

## 5. Lever pile-on

**Symptom:** Three or more levers in short copy. Reader senses the push and recoils. The piece reads as anxious, desperate, or hard-selling — even when individual lines are fine.

**Cause:** Insecurity. *"If one lever isn't enough, more must be better."* Or: writer didn't trust the dominant lever and added belt-and-suspenders levers as backup.

**Fix:**
- Pick one dominant lever based on the diagnosis.
- Cut everything else to supporting at most.
- For short copy, often the right move is one lever, full stop.
- If the writer feels one lever isn't enough, the issue is usually that the lever is poorly chosen, not that more are needed.

---

## 6. Hero-of-own-marketing

**Symptom:** *"We do this. We offer that. Our team. Our process. We've been in business for 20 years. We pride ourselves on…"* Brand is the hero of its own marketing. Customer is barely present.

**Cause:** Forgot Miller. Wrote from the brand's perspective instead of the customer's. Often happens when marketing is written by the founder or by someone proud of the brand's history.

**Fix:**
- Rewrite with the customer as the hero, brand as the guide.
- Watch the pronouns. If "we" appears more than "you," the balance is wrong.
- The brand's history goes in About pages, not hero copy.
- Apply Miller's test: *in 5 seconds, can the reader tell what you offer, why they should care, and what to do next?*

---

## 7. Unfiltered hook

**Symptom:** Hook works for everyone, which means it works for no one in particular. No one feels addressed. Often vague, often inclusive, often emotionally tepid.

**Cause:** Forgot Kennedy. Tried to be inclusive instead of magnetic. Optimized for "doesn't exclude anyone" instead of "draws the right reader in hard."

**Fix:**
- Add a filter. Name the audience explicitly when targeting is broad.
- Make the wrong reader scroll past. *"Real estate agents in their first three years"* is a sharper hook than *"if you're in real estate."*
- Apply Kennedy's test: *does this hook filter — does the right reader stop and the wrong one scroll past?*

---

## 8. Faux-clever

**Symptom:** Tries to be witty, ends up reading as a brand trying to sound clever. Wordplay without substance. Cleverness for cleverness's sake. Reader recognizes the move and disengages.

**Cause:** Forgot Bernbach. Wit isn't decoration — wit is honesty + dry tone delivered with confidence. Cleverness without honesty reads as performance.

**Fix:**
- Cut the cleverness.
- Find the obvious true thing — especially the obvious inconvenient truth nobody else in the category will say.
- Say it dryly.
- Apply Bernbach's test: *does this sound like a person, or a brand?*

---

## 9. No real offer

**Symptom:** The reader finishes the copy and isn't sure what they're supposed to do or get. The CTA exists but the value is fuzzy. The piece pitched a topic, not an offer.

**Cause:** Forgot Brunson. Or: the writer was uncomfortable making a direct ask and softened the offer into a topic.

**Fix:**
- Name what the reader gets, specifically, with friction removed.
- Free + specific + now beats vague.
- The CTA should answer "what happens when I click."
- Apply Brunson's test: *is there an actual offer, or just a topic?*

---

## 10. Pre-frame mismatch

**Symptom:** The opening primes one mental state, the body delivers another. Reader feels bait-and-switched, even subtly. Trust drops mid-piece.

**Cause:** Wrote the body first, then bolted on a hook that doesn't match. Or: changed the body during edits without updating the pre-frame.

**Fix:**
- Either rewrite the hook to honestly pre-frame the body, or rewrite the body to deliver what the hook promised.
- The pre-frame must be true to the offer.
- If you can't make them match, one of them is dishonest.

---

## 11. Objections unaddressed

**Symptom:** The reader has obvious objections; the copy ignores them. Reader walks away without acting, often without being able to articulate why.

**Cause:** Wishful thinking. Hoping the reader won't notice the obvious doubt. Or: writer didn't want to "give the reader ideas" by naming objections.

**Fix:**
- List the top 3 objections the reader is likely to have.
- Address each one with Acknowledge → Reframe → Evidence.
- The "give them ideas" fear is misplaced — readers already have the objections; ignoring them doesn't make them go away, it just leaves them unaddressed.
- For longer copy, dedicate a section to objections explicitly.

---

## How to use this reference

When you spot something off in a draft:

1. Identify the symptom in the table.
2. Read the deeper cause to understand *why* the failure happened.
3. Apply the fix.
4. Re-check after rewriting — sometimes one failure mode masks another.

The most common failure stack: **wrong-stage writing** (skipped diagnostic) leads to **hype** (defaulted to clichés) leads to **lever pile-on** (added more clichés thinking that would fix it). The fix is always upstream — re-do the diagnostic.
