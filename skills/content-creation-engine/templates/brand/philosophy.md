# Content Philosophy — Graeham Watts

Writing principles that apply to every piece of content, regardless of format. Borrowed and adapted from Sabrina Ravanaugh's public framework (April 2026 observation), adapted for real estate BOFU content.

## The four principles

### 1. Consequence-first

Open with what goes WRONG if the viewer doesn't know this. Safe content gets ignored — risk creates attention.

**For real estate:** the consequence is almost always money (overpaying, missing an equity window, listing wrong, choosing the wrong agent). The consequence can also be time (missing a market window) or regret (choosing the wrong house).

**Good opening:** "You've been skipping East Palo Alto because of a 1992 headline. You're paying Palo Alto prices for a problem that stopped existing in 2024."

**Bad opening:** "Let's talk about East Palo Alto real estate." (No consequence, no reason to keep watching.)

### 2. Story over explanation

Don't list features — write scenes. The dinner scenario beats "this agent can manage your transaction."

**For real estate:** put the buyer/seller in a specific moment. The moment they realize their home value is X. The moment an inspector finds the thing. The moment an offer gets accepted.

**Good:** "You're at your kitchen table. Three offers in front of you. The highest isn't the one you should take. Here's why."

**Bad:** "When you get multiple offers, you should consider various factors."

### 3. Escalating stakes

Each segment of the video must feel HIGHER-stakes than the last. If stakes flatten, viewers leave.

For a 4-minute video:
- **Minute 1 stakes:** curiosity (what's the story?)
- **Minute 2 stakes:** money (what's the data?)
- **Minute 3 stakes:** personal (what does this mean for MY home / MY buying decision?)
- **Minute 4 stakes:** urgency (what happens if I don't act?)

If you cut or restructure a segment and it doesn't feel higher-stakes than the segment before it, it doesn't belong in the video.

### 4. Every segment boundary is a retention cliff

The last 2-3 sentences of every segment must make the next segment feel irresistible. This is where viewers decide to stay or leave.

**Good transition:** "…and that's why the price spread matters. But here's what most buyers don't realize about that spread: it closes fast once the narrative catches up. Here's the math on how fast."

**Bad transition:** "Now let's talk about something else."

## Required elements (for YouTube long-form)

Every script must include:
1. **2 personal stories** — real scenarios, 2-3 sentences each. Put one in the hook area, one in the middle.
2. **3 key learnings** — introduced throughout, recapped explicitly in the final segment.
3. **1 signature analogy** — one memorable comparison that makes the complex thing simple.

## Fair Housing constraints

Non-negotiable, applied AFTER the principles above:
- NEVER describe neighborhoods by demographics
- NEVER rank schools as a selling point
- NEVER use "safe," "good area," "family-friendly," "up-and-coming" as proxy language
- Neighborhood content is limited to: property features, price ranges, market trends, lot sizes, amenities, architecture, housing stock age, HOA, zoning, new development, commute/transit facts, walkability

## Tone

Warm but direct. Data-backed but human. Graeham is speaking peer-to-peer with Peninsula buyers/sellers who are smart and busy. Never condescending, never salesy, never "I'm the expert you should trust me" — always "here's the information, here's how to use it."
