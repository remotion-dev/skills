## Guide: Bulk-Build ChatGPT Ads with an AI Agent
- URL: https://aimarketingacademy.com/library/bulk-build-chatgpt-ads-with-an-ai-agent/
- Published: 2026-05-19

What This Is

People are asking ChatGPT who to hire to buy or sell their home — and OpenAI now lets you run ads inside those conversations. This guide shows you how to build a full ChatGPT Ads campaign in bulk using a Claude Cowork agent, so you can ship a real, ready-to-launch campaign in an afternoon instead of a week.

You’ll answer a short series of questions in a Claude chat, the agent will audit your website and recommend three ad groups built around the moments you most want to show up in, and you’ll walk away with a spreadsheet you can upload directly to ads.openai.com — plus a context-hints document and a setup checklist that close the gaps the upload template leaves open.

Here’s the thing — this isn’t a one-click button. ChatGPT Ads is still new, and there’s real work to do in Ads Manager after the upload. But the agent handles the heavy lifting — the strategy, the copywriting, the targeting language, the image mapping — so you can spend your time on the parts that actually need a human.

How to Use It

BEFORE YOU START

A few things to have in place before you run the agent.

A ChatGPT Ads account. Head to ads.openai.com, log in with your existing ChatGPT account, and apply. Approval typically takes 2-4 business days, so start the application now if you haven’t already.

3-6 square images you own or have permission to use. Headshots, neighborhood landmarks, sold-and-closed homes, lifestyle shots. Square ratio (1:1), at least 640×640, no larger than 1200×1200. PNG or JPG. More on image strategy in Step 4.

Your website URL and a clear sense of your business. The agent will audit your site, but the intake questions ask about your market, niches, price range, and goals. Have your numbers handy.

HOW CHATGPT ADS WORK

Two minutes of background so the rest of this guide makes sense.

Three objectives, you pick one per campaign:

Clicks (CPC) — you pay when someone clicks. Recommended starting max bid: $3-5 per click. This is the default we recommend for most agents because it ties spend to measurable engagement.

Reach (CPM) — you pay per 1,000 impressions. Default max bid is $60 CPM. Use this when the goal is brand exposure — getting your name in front of people, not chasing clicks today.

Conversions (CPC) — like Clicks, but optimized toward specific actions on your site (form fills, page views, etc.). Requires installing OpenAI’s tracking pixel on your website first, similar to a Facebook pixel. Worth it if you have conversion events worth optimizing toward and can install the snippet — but installation is on you.

The structure: campaign → ad groups → ads. One campaign holds your objective, budget, dates, and country targeting. Inside that campaign you build ad groups — each ad group is a different moment you want to show up in (a first-time buyer searching for an agent, a luxury seller researching marketing approaches, an investor sourcing deals). Inside each ad group you build the actual ads — title, copy, image, landing page URL.

Targeting is different. The only targeting input is what OpenAI calls a context hint — a plain-language description of the conversations and moments where your ad should appear. The system reads your context hint, your ad copy, and your landing page, then decides whether to show your ad alongside a live ChatGPT conversation. That’s it.

THE BULK TEMPLATE HAS A FLAW

This is the part nobody tells you. The bulk upload template OpenAI provides (currently) has a keywords field at the ad group level — but there’s no such thing as keywords inside the actual Ads Manager. The platform runs entirely on context hints. The keywords field on the spreadsheet is a vestigial leftover that doesn’t map cleanly to what you’ll see in the UI.

So the play is two layers:

The Cowork agent fills the keywords field with strong, ad-group-specific keyword arrays derived from each context hint. This gets the upload through and gives the matcher something to read on import.

You paste the context hint into each ad group in Ads Manager after upload. This is the real targeting input — the field the platform actually uses. Takes about 90 seconds per ad group.

The agent delivers both layers — the spreadsheet ready for upload, and a separate document with the context hints ready to paste. Keywords get the campaign imported. Context hints make it work.

Click Add Skill or Upload Skill.

If you only upload the SKILL.md and leave the subfolder behind, the skill won’t work. Drag the whole folder, or zip the skill folder first and upload that.

Confirm the upload — Claude will show the skill as installed.

STEP 4: DROP YOUR IMAGES INTO THE WORKING FOLDER

Put 3-6 square images into the images/ folder inside the working folder. The agent will look at each image (yes, actually look — it uses vision to recognize what’s in the photo) and recommend which image goes with which ad group.

What works:

Your professional headshot — works for almost any ad group. Pairs naturally with first-time buyer, seller, and “find the right agent” moments where trust is the lever.

Recognizable neighborhood landmarks — a downtown shot, a known building, a familiar street view. Signals local expertise without making property-specific claims.

Sold-and-closed homes (with prior client’s permission) — shows your track record. Won’t get you in trouble because the home is no longer on the market.

Lifestyle shots that anchor your market — main street, well-known restaurant, downtown skyline. Geography without inventory implications.

What to avoid: current active listings. If you use a photo of a home that’s still on the market, and that home goes under contract or sells while your ad is running, you may be misrepresenting current availability. That’s a Fair Housing and consumer protection problem. Stick to sold-and-closed homes if you want to feature property photos, or use neighborhood and lifestyle shots that aren’t tied to a specific transaction.

One more thing on images. The bulk template requires an image URL, not a file upload. That means each image needs to live somewhere public on the internet — your website, Google Drive (set to “anyone with the link”), Dropbox, or a CDN. The agent will recommend the image mapping; you’ll still need to host the images and paste the URLs into Ads Manager after upload. The setup checklist explains exactly how to do this.

STEP 5: RUN THE COWORK AGENT

Open Claude, switch to Cowork mode, and point it at your working folder. Tell the agent: “Build my ChatGPT Ads campaign.”

The agent will:

Read your market-config.md to load your business context.

Audit your website — homepage plus any specialization pages it can find (buyer services, seller services, home valuation, about, niche pages).

Look at each image in your images/ folder and identify what’s in it.

Recommend three ad groups based on your specializations, the landing pages you actually have, and the moments most worth your spend. It’ll show you the picks and explain the rationale.

Wait for your approval. You can swap, edit, or accept.

Write three ads per ad group — title (24 character max), copy (48 character max), landing page URL, image mapping.

Generate the context hint for each ad group plus a strong keyword array for the bulk upload.

Produce three files in the outputs/ folder.

End to end, about 15-25 minutes depending on how chatty you are during the approval step.

context-hints-to-paste.docx — a Word document with one section per ad group. Each section has the context hint paragraph in a styled callout, ready to copy and paste into Ads Manager after the upload imports.

STEP 7: UPLOAD AND LAUNCH IN ADS MANAGER

Log into ads.openai.com. Create a new campaign using the bulk upload option. Drag in your campaign_workbook.xlsx. Wait for it to import — usually under a minute.

Then, for each ad group:

Open the ad group in Ads Manager.

Open context-hints-to-paste.docx. Copy the context hint paragraph for that ad group.

Paste it into the ad group’s targeting field. Save.

For each ad in that ad group, upload the image you mapped to it (using the image-to-ad-group mapping in the setup checklist). Paste the hosted URL into the ad’s image field.

Confirm the landing page URL is correct.

Confirm your max bid is set ($3-5 CPC if you’re going with Clicks objective).

Repeat for all three ad groups. Then review the campaign once at the top level, confirm budget and dates, and launch.

YOUR WEBSITE IS DOING HALF THE WORK

Here’s something most agents miss: your ad is the doorway. Your website is the room. If the room is messy, you’re paying for clicks that bounce.

The matcher reads your landing page as a signal — if the page doesn’t reinforce the context hint, match quality drops and your ad gets served less. And even when it does get served, a slow or unclear landing page kills your conversion rate. So before you spend a dollar on ChatGPT ads, make sure every landing page the agent recommends has:

A clear value proposition above the fold (someone should know what you do in five seconds)

A single primary call-to-action — book a call, request a valuation, contact me — not five competing options

Your contact info visible without scrolling

A page that loads in under 3 seconds (test it at pagespeed.web.dev)

A mobile experience that doesn’t break

The agent’s audit will flag missing specialization pages and downshift to the best available alternative. But if your homepage is also weak, the downshift won’t save you. This is the giant leak in most agents’ boats — and it has nothing to do with ChatGPT Ads. Fix the room before you build more doorways.

COMPLIANCE: KNOW THE LAWS OF YOUR LAND

Real estate advertising is regulated, and the rules differ by country and even by state or province. The agent is built with compliance guardrails — it won’t target or reference protected classes, won’t make claims it can’t substantiate, and won’t omit required disclosures. But the final responsibility is yours. A quick reminder of the major regimes:

United States: Fair Housing Act (no targeting or implied preference for protected classes — race, color, religion, national origin, sex, familial status, disability), RESPA (no referral kickbacks for settlement services), state-level advertising rules (brokerage name often required in ads, team vs. individual agent rules vary).

Canada: CREA Code of Ethics and provincial real estate council rules (RECO in Ontario, OACIQ in Québec, etc.), anti-discrimination provisions in provincial human rights codes, Competition Act on misleading advertising.

Australia: state-specific real estate licensing acts (NSW Property and Stock Agents Act, Victorian Estate Agents Act, etc.), Australian Consumer Law (no misleading or deceptive conduct), anti-discrimination acts at federal and state level.

New Zealand: Real Estate Authority (REA) rules of conduct, Fair Trading Act, Human Rights Act anti-discrimination provisions.

If your brokerage has an ad review process, run your final copy through that before launching. The agent’s output is a strong starting draft — your compliance officer is the final word. The onus is on you to know your jurisdiction’s rules.

Tips

You have two real levers: budget and bids. Budget is the ceiling — raise it and your campaign has more room to spend; lower it and the spend slows. Bids are how that budget gets allocated across ad groups. Each ad group competes for impressions inside the campaign’s shared budget, and the higher its max bid, the more often it wins. Bids can’t exceed what your budget will support, so keep the two in sync. If one ad group isn’t getting impressions after 24-48 hours, bump that ad group’s bid by $1 — that’s your lever to redirect more of the budget toward it.

Three ad groups is the sweet spot. Run more and you fragment your spend across too many moments to learn from any of them. Run fewer and you don’t have enough variety to find what’s working. Three keeps it focused.

Three ads per ad group is the sweet spot too. Each ad should be a different angle — a question, a stat, an objection-disarm. Not the same idea reworded. The matcher gets stronger signal from real variety.

Build the landing pages you don’t have. The agent’s audit will flag every specialization page you’re missing — a luxury seller page, a first-time buyer page, a relocation page. Those are build assignments. A niche-targeted ad sending traffic to a generic homepage is the fastest way to waste money. Every missing page is a conversion drag.

Re-run the agent quarterly. Your market changes. Your inventory changes. Your priorities change. Once your market-config.md is set up, regenerating a fresh campaign takes 15 minutes. Use it.

Watch your spend the first two weeks. Treat the first 30 days as diagnostic, not performance optimization. Look at which ad groups are getting impressions, which are getting clicks, and which landing pages are converting. Pause what’s not working, double down on what is.

## Prompts: ChatGPT Ads Context Hint Library

What This Is

People now ask ChatGPT who they should hire to sell their house or help them buy one. OpenAI lets you run ads below those conversations — and this page gives you 25 ready-to-paste context hints, plus sample headlines and descriptions for each, so most of your campaign is already built.

Getting an ad account. Head to ads.openai.com, log in with your existing ChatGPT account, and apply. Approval isn’t instant, so start the process now if you haven’t already.

The structure. Inside a campaign, you build ad groups. Each ad group gets two inputs: one landing page URL and one context hint. Run as many ad groups as you want — one for buyers, one for sellers, one for investors — each targeting a different moment.

What’s a context hint? A short paragraph (2-4 sentences) describing the person and the moment you want your ad to show up in. Not a keyword list. A description.

The formula: Persona (who they are) + Location (always local) + Intent (what they’re trying to do) + Moment (timing or trigger event).

Landing pages and ad copy. Each hint below comes with a recommended landing page (and a downshift if you don’t have the ideal one yet) plus 3 sample headlines and 2 sample descriptions. The copy is written to filter for high-intent leads — people ready to talk, not still researching. Lower volume, better quality.

How to Use It

Pick four to six context hints that match your business. Don’t try to run all 25 — you’ll fragment your spend and dilute your testing. Build a dedicated landing page for each one (or use the best fallback you have), paste the hint into the Context Hints field in your ad group, drop in the sample headlines and descriptions, and point your ad at the right URL. Test, measure, refine.

The hints are organized into three sections: Positioning Moments (someone looking for an agent), Seller Moments, and Buyer Moments. Swap your city, metro, or neighborhood into the bracketed placeholders before you paste.

Tips

Don’t run all 25 at once. Pick four to six that match your business. Trying to test everything simultaneously fragments your spend and dilutes the signal.

Build the landing page before you launch the ad. Sending a niche-targeted ad to a generic homepage is the fastest way to waste money. The matcher reads your landing page as a signal — if it doesn’t reinforce the hint, match quality drops. Every specialization page you don’t have yet is a build assignment.

Vary your headlines and descriptions intentionally. Each one should be a different angle — a question, a specific number, an objection-disarm, a callout — not the same idea reworded. The matcher gets stronger signal from real variety.

Keep your image simple and on-message. OpenAI recommends clear, uncluttered visuals that match what the headline and description are saying. A friendly headshot, a clean shot of a local home, or a recognizable neighborhood landmark works. Skip stock photos of generic skylines.

Fair Housing matters here too. These hints and sample headlines are written to target housing situations and financial moments, not protected classes. Don’t modify them to reference family status, marital status, age, religion, or anything else covered by Fair Housing. Target the housing need, not the household type.

What This Is

You’re setting up an AI agent that writes branded, hyperlocal market report emails for your database. One email per geographic segment — by ZIP, city, county, subdivision, or neighborhood — paired with a tagged recipient list. You paste each email into a new campaign in your email service provider, send, and you’re done.

Imagine you have 250 contacts spread across five ZIP codes in your market. Today, sending each of them a market update means either writing one generic email that’s too broad to land, or manually building five hyperlocal versions and figuring out who gets which. This AI agent does the latter — automatically. It reads your contacts, identifies the geographies they represent, asks you for the matching MLS data, and produces a polished email per geography with real numbers from your local market. Median prices, days on market, list-to-sale ratios, inventory levels — calculated from the actual MLS data you provide.

What’s new vs. the original:

Dual perspective in every email. The original wrote seller-only reports. Every email this version produces includes both a seller perspective (“If you’re thinking about selling…”) and a buyer perspective (“If you’re looking to buy in this area…”) — same market data, framed two ways. So a homeowner who gets the email for their own ZIP sees both what their home could fetch and what buyers there are facing. And if your CRM also shows that same contact is searching to buy in a different area, they’d get a second email for that area — with both POVs of that market.

Smarter geographic segmentation. The original was ZIP-code only. This version lets you choose any geographic granularity your market makes sense at — ZIP, city, county, subdivision, neighborhood, or even a custom MLS field. You configure your preferred segmentation at intake.

Filters that focus the campaign. Layer property type (single-family, condo, luxury, etc.), price range, contact source, or buyer/seller emphasis onto your segmentation. Keep the campaign focused and the email count manageable.

Tips

Bring fresh data each run. Re-export your contacts CSV and pull a fresh MLS report before each campaign. The whole point is hyperlocal relevance — stale data undermines that.

Your data stays local. Contacts, MLS exports, and generated emails are saved to a folder on your computer. Nothing gets uploaded anywhere.

You stay in control of sending. The AI agent generates emails and recipient lists. You import them into your email platform and schedule the send. Nothing auto-sends.

Always review before sending. AI-generated market insights can occasionally pick up on patterns that aren’t statistically meaningful or that need additional context. Review every email before you schedule a campaign.

Watch your MLS data quality. Garbage in, garbage out. If your MLS export has bad data (missing prices, incorrect ZIPs, mis-categorized properties), the emails will reflect that. Pull clean data with a defined time window — typically the last 90–180 days.

One AI agent, one campaign. Don’t try to make a single AI agent handle multiple unrelated campaigns. If you want sellers and buyers as separate campaigns, or Brentwood and Williamson as separate campaigns, spin up multiple AI agents — each with its own folder, config, and kickoff prompt.
