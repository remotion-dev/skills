# Shot Glossary — plain English

Every Call Sheet includes this glossary at the top of the **videographer packet**, and every shot direction in the packet is written in **plain English first**, with the abbreviation in parentheses only as shorthand. The goal: a videographer (or a stand-in) can read a line cold and know exactly what to do without decoding jargon. Never write "MS push-in" alone — write "Medium shot (waist-up), camera slowly moves toward him (push-in)."

## Shot size — how much of the subject/house is in frame

| Term (abbr.) | Plain English |
|---|---|
| Wide shot (WS) | The whole subject with room around it. For a house: the full structure corner-to-corner with space above and beside it. For a person: head to feet with background. |
| Medium shot (MS) | Waist-up on a person. For a room: one zone of it, not the whole space. |
| Medium close-up (MCU) | Chest-up on a person — the standard talking-head framing. |
| Close-up (CU) | One feature fills the frame — a faucet, a sign, hands, a face. |
| Extreme close-up (ECU) | A tiny detail fills the frame — a tap turning, a logo, an eye. |
| Establishing shot | A wide shot at the start that shows where we are (the house, the street, the block). |

## Camera movement — what the camera physically does

| Term | Plain English |
|---|---|
| Static / locked off | Camera does not move. On a tripod. |
| Push-in | Camera slowly moves *toward* the subject (gets closer). Not a zoom — the camera physically advances. |
| Pull-back / pull-out | Camera slowly moves *away*, revealing more around the subject. |
| Gimbal walk | The operator walks while holding a gimbal (a motorized stabilizer handle), so the moving shot stays smooth, not shaky. "Gimbal walk forward toward the door." |
| Pan | Camera stays in place and turns left or right. |
| Tilt | Camera stays in place and pivots up or down. "Tilt up the front of the house to the roofline." |
| Orbit / arc | Camera moves in a curve *around* the subject, keeping it centered. |
| Dolly | Camera moves on a straight line (on wheels/slider) — left, right, forward, back. |
| Drone / aerial | Shot from a drone, looking down or pulling back from above. |
| Reveal | Any move that starts hiding something and ends showing it (pull-back reveal, tilt-up reveal). |

## Camera height & angle — where the camera sits

| Term | Plain English |
|---|---|
| Eye-level | Camera at a standing adult's eye height. The neutral default. |
| Low angle | Camera near the ground looking up. Makes a house/subject feel taller and grander. |
| High angle | Camera above looking down. Good for showing layout (a yard, a patio, a floor plan). |
| Chest height | Camera ~chest high — common for smooth gimbal walking shots. |
| Overhead / top-down | Straight down from above. |

## Composition cues — how to frame within the shot

Use these to tell the videographer *exactly* how to place things, especially for exteriors:

- **How much of the structure:** "full front of the house, corner to corner" vs "just the entry and porch" vs "the right third where the ADU sits."
- **Sky vs ground ratio:** "roughly 20% sky above the roofline, house in the middle, lawn filling the bottom third" — this stops a videographer from cutting off the roof or burying the house in pavement.
- **Where to stand:** name the spot — "from the end of the front walk," "from the dining-room threshold," "from the back-right corner of the yard."
- **What light:** "golden hour, sun behind camera so the front is lit," or "open shade, no harsh shadows."
- **Headroom / lead room** (people): "chest-up, a little space above the head, Graeham centered."

Every exterior and hero interior shot in the packet must answer: *how much of the thing is in frame, from where, at what height, with how much sky/ground, in what light.* If a line doesn't answer those, it isn't finished.
